U.S. Power Grid · 2026 Interconnection Outlook

Everything wants to plug in. Almost nothing can.

Roughly 600 GW of generation and storage is targeting 2026 first power across the seven ISOs and six large non-ISO utilities — more than half the installed U.S. fleet. The queue is the bottleneck: a national average wait of 4.9 years, capacity prices up 10× where supply is tightest, and a fuel mix tilting hard toward batteries and gas as renewables tax credits sunset.

01 / National

The interconnection funnel

The setup

The headline number — ~600 GW targeting 2026 — overstates what will actually energize. Projects sit in tiers of decreasing probability: a large early queue that has not cleared studies, a study stage working through impact and facilities analysis, and a much smaller under-construction band that is the only near-certain 2026 supply. Read the funnel, not the headline.

From queue to steel in the ground
GW by maturity tier within the ~600 GW targeting 2026 first power
Under construction (~95 GW) is the highest-probability tier. The early queue and study-stage volumes carry attrition rates that historically exceed 70%.

02 / National

Time to power, by region

Why it matters

The single most decisive variable is how long a project waits. ERCOT clears in 2.9 years on a connect-and-manage model; the RTOs that run full cluster studies — CAISO at 8.5, PJM at 7.2 — take roughly three times as long. That gap is why ERCOT keeps adding capacity while load-growth regions struggle to keep up.

03 / National

Not all fuels wait the same

Fuel detail

Within a region the spread by fuel is brutal. Gas and battery clear fastest where the market favors dispatchable capacity; wind is punished everywhere — 13.7 years in PJM, 11.7 in SPP — while it sails through ERCOT and MISO. The queue is, in effect, a policy instrument: it rewards what the grid says it needs.

What's coming online

The buildout

Stack the 2026 queue by fuel and the story is storage and gas. PJM alone carries ~67 GW of queued battery and ~39 GW of gas; ERCOT and AZPS are battery-dominated; the Southeast (Duke, TVA) is the gas heartland. The under-construction view — the near-certain slice — is far smaller and tells you who actually delivers in 2026.

The price signal

Scarcity

Where the queue can't keep up with load, capacity markets scream. PJM's 2026/27 auction cleared at $329.17/MW-day — roughly 10× the 2024 result. MISO touched $666.50 in summer scarcity zones. These are the clearest market signals that interconnection delay has become a reliability problem, not just a developer headache.

07 / Policy

The rules are moving too

Federal

Two 2025 laws reset the federal frame. OBBBA (July 2025) phases out the wind/solar PTC & ITC on a hard clock — a physical-work / 5%-safe-harbor deadline of July 4, 2026 and a placed-in-service cliff of Dec 31, 2027 — while leaving the grid-scale battery ITC intact through 2036. The GRID Act (Sept 2025) mandates FERC interconnection-timeline reform and tilts toward dispatchable capacity. Together they explain the fuel mix: a rush to safe-harbor renewables now, and a structural tailwind for storage and gas after.

10 / Access

Two front doors to the grid

The models

Everything above assumes one way onto the grid: file an interconnection request, wait for the study, build the upgrades, energize. That process is built around firm service — it sizes the grid for a load drawing 100% of its maximum in every hour, including the system peak — so a single large data-center request tends to trigger the full slate of network upgrades whose lead times now run into years.

There is a second door. Instead of asking the grid to guarantee every megawatt in every hour, the load agrees to back off during the handful of stressed hours each year. ERCOT calls its version connect-and-manage; FERC codified the same idea as Energy Resource Interconnection Service. The trade is simple — give up firm capacity rights, accept some curtailment, and skip to the front of the queue.

FERC · firm service
Study, upgrade, then connect
FERC's pro-forma process assumes a new load is firm — drawing its full maximum at all times, including system-wide peaks. The Duke study notes this is "far more likely to trigger the need for significant upgrades, such as new transformers, transmission line reconductoring, circuit breakers, or other substation equipment."
Transformer lead times have ballooned to 2–5 years (costs +80%); high-voltage circuit breakers hit 151 weeks in late 2023. Firm service waits on every bit of it.
ERCOT · connect-and-manage
Connect first, curtail the few stressed hours
ERCOT's 2022 interim large-load process lets a data center qualify as a controllable load resource (CLR) — "studied as flexible and potentially interconnect more MWs." FERC's Energy Resource Interconnection Service (ERIS, Order 2003) is the parallel: forgo firm capacity rights and accept curtailment risk in exchange for "expedited lower-cost grid access."
ERCOT: "the optimal solution for grid reliability is for more loads to participate in economic dispatch as CLRs" — and its grid could absorb on the order of 10 GW of new flexible load, among the top five BAs nationally.
So how much new load can the existing grid absorb if it's willing to flex? A Duke study put numbers on it.
Curtailment-enabled headroom
The grid is built for a peak it rarely hits — leaving room underneath.
Across the 22 largest US balancing authorities (≈95% of national peak demand), new load that agrees to be curtailed for a sliver of the year fits inside the system's existing headroom — with no new generation or transmission. The more curtailment the load tolerates, the more of it the grid can swallow.

Which is exactly the gap a battery fills.
The unlock
Batteries make the curtailment window invisible.
The grid only needs the load to ease off for ~22 hours a year, in stretches averaging under two — and nearly 90% of those hours still want at least half the load. An on-site battery rides through exactly that shortfall: the data center stays firm to its servers while presenting as flexible to the grid. That is why colocating storage behind a flexible interconnection — rather than waiting years for firm capacity — is the fast path on.
FERC's late-2024 colocation conference made the point directly: the Clean Energy Buyers Association called the flexibility of co-located demand "a key asset that can enable rapid, reliable interconnection."

11 / Synthesis

Why the queue ends in batteries

The logic

Pull every thread above together and a single causal chain explains the storage surge. New load — overwhelmingly AI data centers — wants firm power on a timeline measured in months. The grid answers on a timeline measured in years. Batteries are the only resource that resolves both halves of that mismatch at once, which is why they top the 2026 queue in ERCOT, PJM, CAISO and AZPS alike.

The trigger
A wave of data-center load wants firm power — now.
Hyperscale AI build-outs arrive in single requests measured in hundreds of megawatts to gigawatts, and they want to energize in months, not years.
…but the grid can't deliver on that timeline, for two reasons.
Blocker · 01
Interconnection gridlock
A new plant joins a queue that takes years to clear — and a transmission upgrade can take longer still.
Queue waits run 7.2 yrs in PJM and 8.5 yrs in CAISO against a 4.9-yr national average.
Blocker · 02
Capacity is already short
Where load outpaces what can connect, scarcity shows up directly in the capacity price.
PJM cleared $329/MW-day (≈10×); MISO summer scarcity touched $666.
So load needs a resource defined by two properties at once:
The requirement
Fast to energize and dispatchable.
Speed clears the interconnection problem; firmness clears the reliability problem. A resource that delivers only one of the two doesn't break the logjam.
Batteries are the resource that satisfies both — for three structural reasons.
Speed
Modular & quick to build
A storage project is sited and energized in months, sidestepping the multi-year wait a new gas plant or transmission line requires.
Policy
ITC retained to 2036
OBBBA phases out the wind & solar credits on a hard 2026–27 clock but leaves the grid-scale battery ITC intact — a decade-long subsidy edge.
Access
Connect-and-manage & peak-shaving
Flexible interconnection (ERCOT's model) and behind-the-meter siting let storage plug in without waiting on the full network upgrade.
The outcome
Batteries lead the 2026 interconnection queue.
PJM 66.9 GW · AZPS 22.2 GW · ERCOT 30.9 GW · CAISO 20.1 GW queued — storage is the single largest fuel in the national queue.
